The Amplification Paradox

Introduction

Recommendation systems are a crucial part of today's digital world, connecting users with content that aligns with their interests. However, there is an ongoing debate about the role algorithms play in promoting extreme content, which can have negative consequences for users and society. The "amplification paradox" highlights the contradiction between the exposure to extreme content and the fact that such content is rarely consumed, especially not primarily through algorithmic recommendations.

This blog post aims to explore the amplification paradox, discuss the risks of recommendation systems, and emphasize the importance of understanding user behavior and preferences in their development and evaluation. The post will include an animation explaining how agent-based models can help unravel the paradox, making the complex subject more accessible. Ultimately, this aims to ensure the potential dangers of recommendation systems are acknowledged and addressed responsibly.

The Agent-Based Model

To explore the content recommended by recommendation systems, we developed an agent-based model. This model, which can be interpreted as a bot, selects videos from the suggestions provided by the recommender. We can configure this model to choose videos randomly or to mimic the way people pick videos. To accurately represent people's behavior, we introduce the term "relative utility," which represents the usefulness of each category to the user. Categories with low relative utility for a user will have a smaller chance of being selected by the model.

In the interactive animation below, you can engage with the model and observe how it selects videos from the recommendations provided by the system. You can decide whether you want the model to pick videos randomly or based on utility. When set to random, all categories have an equal chance of being selected, whereas when set to utility, categories have varying chances of being selected depending on the user's preferences. Utility Selection

We've observed how recommender systems tend to amplify extreme content through random selection. But, let's be real, that's not how people actually choose videos. When you're browsing YouTube, you don't just click on random videos, do you? You typically go for the ones that catch your interest. That's what this configuration aims to mimic. We figure out the value of each category for a user, and then the model picks a video based on the combined utilities we've calculated.
